Introduction (Rewritten)
A standard Turing computation can be pictured as a sequence of simple read-write-move steps performed in time by one or more "heads" operating on "tapes." What the machine does at each step is fixed by two things: the contents of the tape at the start, and a finite program that tells the head how to react to what it sees. Different choices about the alphabet, the set of primitive operations, or the tape layout might affect how fast a computation runs or how much space it uses, but the underlying notion of computability stays the same — this is the content of the Church-Turing thesis. Without loss of generality, then, we can fix our attention on machines whose tapes are indexed by the natural numbers ω = {0, 1, 2, ...} and whose cells hold either a 0 or a 1.
From the standpoint of ordinal theory, there is a natural way to push this picture further: instead of indexing the cells of the tape by ω, index them by the entire class Ord of ordinal numbers. A computation then becomes an Ord-indexed sequence of elementary tape operations, where each ordinal plays the role of a moment in time. At successor stages, the machine behaves exactly as a finite Turing machine would. At limit stages — where there is no immediate predecessor moment to read off — we need a separate rule. We define the tape contents, the program state, and the head's location at a limit time by suitable limit operations, specifically by taking inferior limits of the values that appeared at earlier stages.
The resulting notion of ordinal computability clearly subsumes ordinary Turing computability. Invoking the Church-Turing thesis on the natural numbers, every function on ω that is Turing computable is also ordinal computable. More than that, the standard arithmetic of ordinals — addition, multiplication, exponentiation — together with various other ordinal operations turn out to be ordinal computable as well.
In fact, the class of ordinal computable functions is rich enough that, with a suitable coding scheme, even the bounded truth predicate
{(α, φ, x) | α ∈ Ord, φ an ∈-formula, x ∈ L_α, L_α ⊨ φ(x)}
for Gödel's constructible hierarchy L = ⋃_{α ∈ Ord} L_α can be computed by an ordinal Turing machine. From this we obtain the central theorem of the paper:
Theorem 1. A set x ⊆ Ord is ordinal computable from finitely many ordinal parameters if and only if x ∈ L.
The "only if" direction is essentially a matter of absoluteness: ordinal computations are absolute enough that any computation producing x can be carried out inside L itself. The "if" direction takes more work — one has to show that the level-by-level construction of the constructible hierarchy can actually be simulated by an ordinal Turing machine, which involves a careful look at how that hierarchy is built up.
In spirit, Theorem 1 plays a role analogous to the Church-Turing thesis itself: it tells us that ordinal computability picks out a natural, robust, and absolute family of sets, one that does not depend sensitively on the particular formalism used to define it.
Frameworks for transfinite computation are not new. A prominent example is E-recursion, treated at length in Sacks' monograph [6], which assigns a computational value {e}(x) to programs e built from basic set-theoretic operations applied to arbitrary sets. The link between E-computability and the constructible universe runs roughly parallel to Theorem 1 above. Computer science has also produced its own family of infinitary machines, such as Büchi automata [1], with substantial applications. What distinguishes the present approach is that it stays close to Turing's original picture — a head reading and writing on an unrestricted tape — and reads "unrestricted" in the strongest possible sense, namely as set-theoretic unboundedness.
The direct inspiration for this work is the infinite time Turing machine of Hamkins, Kidder, and Lewis [4]. Their machines use ordinary ω-length tapes but are allowed to run for transfinite numbers of steps, with tape contents at limit times defined as inferior limits of earlier contents. Because inputs and outputs are characteristic functions on ω, they are naturally identified with real numbers, and the resulting theory connects closely to descriptive set theory and definability over (ℝ, ...). Allowing the tape itself to have arbitrary ordinal length shifts the natural setting: instead of definability over the reals, one is led to a theory of arbitrarily iterated definitions — that is, to the theory of constructibility.
